**Topic:** *Handling Multiple Speakers (Discussion) & Lectures (Monologue)*.
**Exam Relevance:**

* **Section 3:** A discussion between 2-4 people (often students and a tutor). The challenge is tracking **who thinks what** and identifying **agreement/disagreement**.
* **Section 4:** A university lecture on an academic topic. The challenge is the length (no breaks) and the dense vocabulary.

## 1. üìò Deep Theory: Tracking "Consensus" (Section 3)

In Section 3, you are often asked: *"What do the students agree on?"*
They will discuss an idea, argue, and then settle. You must wait for the **Consensus**.

**The Pattern:**

1. **Proposal:** Student A: *"I think we should focus on the history."*
2. **Rejection:** Student B: *"I'm not sure that's relevant..."* (Distractor).
3. **Counter-Proposal:** Student B: *"...how about the methodology?"*
4. **Agreement:** Student A: *"Actually, yes, that makes more sense."* (Answer: Methodology).

**Key Phrases for Agreement/Disagreement:**

* *I can't go along with that.* (Disagree).
* *You have a point, but...* (Partial Disagree).
* *I suppose so.* (Reluctant Agree).
* *Spot on.* (Strong Agree).

## 2. üìò Deep Theory: Lecture Signposting (Section 4)

In Section 4, the speaker talks for 5-7 minutes without stopping. You will get lost if you don't listen for **Signposts**. These words tell you when the speaker moves to the next question.

* **Moving on:** *"Turning now to the issue of..."* (Next question).
* **Detailing:** *"Let's look at this in more depth."*
* **Contrasting:** *"On the other hand, traditional theories suggest..."*
* **Concluding:** *"So, what this tells us is..."* (Answer usually follows).
